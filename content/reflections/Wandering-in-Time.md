**31 December 2025 – Wednesday – 13:33**  
  
**Wandering in Time**  
  
Last year, in these very moments and days, I could not form any clear image in my mind of where I would be at this exact time the following year.  
I had stepped onto a path filled with excitement, motivation, hope, fear, ambiguity, and an uneven mixture of many other emotions.  
  
I did not know what shape or course it would take.  
Where I would be a year later.  
In what coordinates, in what atmosphere.  
  
Four months have now passed since my arrival in the Netherlands. The speed and the number of events have been far greater than I had imagined.  
  
I am still in a state of shock and astonishment regarding my migration.  
In this new environment, this new city, I am gradually settling in and dissolving into it, yet at the same time accepting and fully digesting the fact that I have now been living in a completely different geography for more than four months remains difficult.  
Accepting and touching the present moment is often hard, and recalling and becoming aware of the path already taken is even harder.  
  
On one side, there is a feeling of guilt and regret regarding some decisions, behaviors, choices, and longings;  
on the other side, feelings of pride, joy, motivation, and hope.  
  
On one side, regret for the many experiences I never had in the past;  
on the other, sorrow for not being realistic and not appreciating the conditions, opportunities, and blessings of my own life.  
  
It seems that in perceiving the realities of life in general, I suffer from a serious weakness and difficulty. I do not have a proper understanding of the course of my own life or the moment I am in, and I usually realize the quality and value of things only after a long time has passed—at which point anger and regret overwhelm me, wondering why I did not understand and absorb my position in that very moment.  
  
With complete boldness, I thought only about leaving and escaping from the city, the country, and the people.  
To cut everything off.  
To sever it all and be free.  
To escape.  
  
Distance. Separation. Freedom.  
  
I was completely certain and decisive that I would never feel homesick. I even mocked the very concept of homesickness.  
I believed I had no attachment to any person, environment, or place, and that migration would be very easy for me—that I would never miss these people, these streets, these crowds, or their anxieties.  
  
Much like what I did years ago when I migrated from Damavand to Tehran.  
Years later I was filled with regret and sorrow and came to the realization that I had been wrong.  
  
Moving away and migrating to Tehran, expanding relationships and progressing in life—that was not the mistake.  
The mistake was the way I thought about it, the imagination and the intention behind it.  
  
I lost certain qualities.  
I weakened and destroyed various relationships.  
A self-chosen distance.  
And I regretted it.  
  
Everything, in practice and on paper, went well.  
Scientific progress, professional advancement, specialization, income—everything.  
  
But long-standing friendships filled with memories and depth faded away and disappeared.  
Better performance, greater achievements—yet often weaker spiritual and emotional quality.  
Fewer attachments. Weaker ones.  
  
The turning point came when one of my close friends died in 2020.  
I had been distant from him for several years.  
The regret for all the days and memories we could have created together during those years—but did not—collapsed upon me, and even now I have not fully digested it. The weight of it has never been lifted from my shoulders.  
  
Now I think that once again I may have taken the same path—only on a far larger scale.  
  
This time I fled from my own country and continent.  
For progress, for growth, for expanding possibilities, experiences, and relationships—and for everything else.  
Yet perhaps also out of deep exhaustion, emotional weariness, and disillusionment with the environment and the people around me.  
  
What if this pattern repeats itself again?  
  
What if in a few years I once again find myself wanting to escape the moment—tired of everything and everyone?  
  
Now I see all the signs of homesickness within myself.  
Unconsciously, at night I dream of relatives, friends, and classmates.  
The same people and gatherings I once seemed to be counting the days to leave behind.  
  
Those same streets and alleys—dirty and anxiety-filled.  
That same numb feeling of driving behind the wheel in Tehran.  
  
I wish I had valued those moments while I was in them.  
I wish I did not make everything so difficult for myself in my mind and in my life.  
  
I am constantly fleeing from routine, stability, and a defined course.  
As soon as a pattern becomes fixed and repetitive, I do everything in my power to change it and disrupt it.  
  
It is as if, unconsciously, despite all my fears and anxieties, I am always trying to push myself outside my safe zone in any possible way.  
  
Apparently this is an important, valuable, even beneficial trait.  
And in truth, it has been.  
  
If I had not possessed the courage—despite all my inner fears and anxieties—to step outside my safe zone and walk into unknown paths, I would not be here now. I would not have done these things. I would not have lived through my particular past experiences.  
  
But I still question it.  
  
Perhaps sometimes a safe zone is necessary.  
Perhaps stability also has its value.  
Perhaps it is good for a person to pause for a moment and rest within a healthy and secure framework, and enjoy it—rather than destroying it in hope of discovering greater joy and excitement in the next stage.  
  
Whatever the case, I am homesick.  
  
Filled with regret that before leaving my country and my city, instead of rushing toward departure and thinking only about getting out, I had paused for a while and enjoyed my final moments more—being beside my family, my friends, my city, and my country.  
  
Not that I necessarily would have done anything special.  
Simply that mentally and emotionally I would have been more aware, and allowed the moments to pass more slowly within my mind—processing and absorbing them.  
  
I would have felt my family, my friends, and my beloved more deeply.  
I would have sat beside them with greater presence of mind and spirit.  
  
I regret nothing.  
  
Everything I have done, I have done willingly.  
  
What disappoints me is only my lack of proper mastery over my emotions, my inner state, and my perspective toward life.  
  
Where will this path lead?  
What will happen?  
  
I do not know.  
And I cannot imagine it.  
  
It feels as if I have already reached and done whatever needed to be done, and now—rather than looking toward the future and its next stages—I find myself looking back at the past.  
  
Perhaps it is a characteristic of this environment: concerns about the future are weaker here than they were in my own country, and now I have unintentionally found space to look more deeply into the past.  
  
I am homesick and alone.  
A deep, inner loneliness.  
Homesick for myself, and for the moments that passed—spent anxiously rushing toward the future without recognizing their true quality.
